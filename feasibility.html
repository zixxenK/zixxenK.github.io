Feasibility Study: Crossroads Community STEM Centre

An Assessment of the Viability of Establishing a Community STEM Learning Hub in Langford, British Columbia

Prepared for: Jasmine North, Department of English, Camosun College

Prepared by: Cameron Kibble, Founder, Crossroads Community Hub

Date: March 26, 2026 (Updated: March 29, 2026)

Year 1 Capital (Lean) $476,100
Annual Operating $310,000
Break-Even Target Month 8
Survey Interest 65%
Member Target 200
Weekly Access Hours 73 hrs

1. Introduction

This feasibility study assesses the viability of establishing the Crossroads Community STEM Centre, a proposed open-access technical learning facility in Langford, British Columbia. The study evaluates the project across eight dimensions - technical, cost, economic, social, environmental, cultural, political/legal, operational, and schedule - to determine whether the project should proceed to the funding acquisition and construction phases.

1.1 Study Scope and Methodology

The assessment applies a structured systems engineering feasibility framework: problem formulation, generation of alternatives, and determination of viability across all applicable dimensions. Each viability dimension concludes with an explicit feasibility determination.

1.2 Data Sources

All data presented herein is drawn from three primary sources. No data has been fabricated or assumed without documentation.

  1. Crossroads Community STEM Centre Project Proposal (March 11, 2026) - the original operational and financial plan containing startup budgets, revenue projections, equipment specifications, staffing models, and risk assessments.
  2. Independent 2026 Local Market Cost Analysis (March 26, 2026) - an updated analysis recalibrating the proposal’s startup estimates against current Langford commercial lease rates, provincial wage benchmarks, and inflationary pressures. Where financial figures conflict between the proposal and this analysis, the updated March 26 figures are treated as canonical.
  3. Community Needs Assessment Survey (February 2026) - a survey of 54 respondents in the Langford and Greater Victoria area measuring demand for accessible STEM programming, preferred disciplines, price sensitivity, and scheduling preferences.

2. Needs Analysis

Langford is Vancouver Island’s fastest-growing municipality, with a 2026 projected population of 63,120 and a 3.95 percent year-over-year growth rate. Despite this expansion, the West Shore region lacks a centralized, open-access facility where residents can develop practical technical skills outside of formal post-secondary enrollment. Access to advanced tools and lab environments in the Greater Victoria area is concentrated within institutions that impose tuition, enrollment, and scheduling barriers.

A review of existing alternatives in the region confirms this gap. The University of Victoria and Camosun College maintain well-equipped STEM labs, but access is restricted to enrolled students during designated institutional hours. The Victoria Makerspace operates on an application-based membership model with limited public drop-in hours and a club-oriented atmosphere focused on a narrow range of hobbyist disciplines.

A community needs assessment survey of 54 respondents in the Langford and Greater Victoria area yielded the following results:

  • 78% identified “lack of affordable access to tools and lab space” as their primary barrier to developing technical skills.
  • 65% indicated they would “definitely” or “probably” use a drop-in community STEM facility in Langford.
  • 61% preferred a monthly membership fee in the $25 to $50 range, though market realities may necessitate adjustments.

The British Columbia Ministry of Education’s Applied Design, Skills, and Technologies (ADST) curriculum emphasizes experiential learning and iterative design across K-12 education. Crossroads directly extends the ADST model into the community setting, bridging the gap between secondary education and professional practice.

3. System Identification

The proposed system is a nonprofit community STEM learning facility located in Langford, BC, operating across four core disciplines: Robotics and Electronics, Digital Fabrication, Software Development, and Creative and Applied Design.

Exogenous Variables

  • Municipal population growth rate and demographic shifts in the West Shore.
  • Langford commercial real estate market lease rates (Net + NNN).
  • Provincial and federal grant funding climate for educational nonprofits.
  • WorkSafeBC regulatory requirements and premium rates for workshop environments.
  • BC Societies Act requirements for nonprofit incorporation.
  • Inflationary pressures on specialized equipment and regional wage benchmarks.

Overt Variables

  • Staffing model and compensation structure.
  • Equipment selection, lifecycle management, and procurement strategy.
  • Programming schedule spanning 73 total access hours.
  • Fee structure encompassing sliding-scale memberships, drop-in passes, and workshop fees.
  • Revenue diversification strategy capping single-source dependencies at 30%.

4. Problem Formulation

Langford, BC, despite its rapid demographic and economic expansion, lacks an open-access community facility for residents to develop practical STEM skills using professional-grade equipment without post-secondary enrollment. Existing alternatives either restrict access to enrolled students or impose application-based membership barriers unsuited to beginners. Consequently, career changers, youth exploring STEM interests, and local professionals lack affordable, structured access to hands-on technical education.

4.2 Demand Quantification

The February 2026 community needs assessment survey (n=54) quantifies demand across four discipline areas:

  • 3D Printing / Digital Fabrication: 72% interest
  • Software Development: 58% interest
  • Robotics / Electronics: 54% interest
  • Creative and Applied Design: 41% interest

Additionally, 68% of respondents preferred weekday evening access and 59% preferred weekend availability, both of which are accommodated by the proposed 73-hour weekly schedule.

5. Generation of System Alternatives

Three system alternatives have been developed to address the problem formulation, representing different risk and investment profiles.

Alternative A - Lean Model

Recommended

4,000 sqft facility focusing on core equipment clusters in 3D printing, electronics, and computing. Prioritizes manageable capital risk and operational agility.

Specifications
  • Startup Capital: $476,100
  • Annual Operating: $310,000
  • Staff: ED + 2 Lab Supervisors (FTE)
  • Access: 73 hrs/week (63 staffed + 10 self-access)
  • Risk Profile: Moderate
  • Scalability: Can scale to Alternative B as revenue stabilizes

Alternative B - Expanded Model

Higher Risk

6,000 sqft facility with full equipment complement including larger CNC router, expanded fabrication lab, and enterprise-grade digital infrastructure.

Specifications
  • Startup Capital: $738,300
  • Annual Operating: $450,000
  • Staff: ED + 3+ Lab Supervisors (FTE)
  • Access: 73+ hrs/week
  • Risk Profile: High (capital exposure)
  • Scalability: Full-scale from launch

Alternative C - Pop-Up Fallback

Fallback Only

Mobile workshop model renting community center space. Risk-mitigation fallback only - cannot deliver continuous equipment access fundamental to the core mission.

Specifications
  • Startup Capital: <$50,000
  • Annual Operating: Variable per event
  • Staff: Contract instructors only
  • Access: Schedule-bound only
  • Risk Profile: Low (no lease commitment)
  • Scalability: Cannot scale to permanent facility

Recommendation: Alternative A is recommended for initial launch. It carries a lower financial risk profile and can sustainably scale to Alternative B as earned revenue stabilizes.

6. Determination of Viability

Eight dimensions of feasibility have been evaluated. Each dimension below includes expandable detail cards with supporting data drawn from the project proposal, the 2026 market analysis, and the community needs survey.

6.1 Technical Feasibility

The required equipment is commercially available and well-documented. The facility requires 200-amp electrical service, industrial ventilation for fabrication areas, and compliance with the Accessible Canada Act. These requirements are standard for light industrial spaces available in Langford’s McCallum Road and West Shore Parkway zones.

Equipment Inventory (Lean)

Confirmed

All equipment is commercially available with established supply chains. Surplus from Camosun CTAC projected to offset $15,000-$30,000.

Full Inventory
  • Robotics/Electronics: 20x Arduino Uno R4, 10x Raspberry Pi 5, 6x soldering stations, 4x oscilloscopes
  • Digital Fabrication: 2-4x FDM 3D printers, 1x 40W CO2 laser cutter, 1x benchtop CNC router
  • Software/Creative: 10-15x workstations, 6x drawing tablets, design software licenses
  • Infrastructure: Component inventory, test benches, networking equipment

Facility Requirements

Standard

Requirements are standard for light industrial spaces available in Langford commercial zones at $16-$22/sqft net + NNN.

Requirements Detail
  • Electrical: 200-amp service with dedicated circuits
  • Ventilation: Industrial ventilation, soldering fume extraction, CNC dust collection
  • ESD Protection: ESD-safe electronics workstations
  • Accessibility: Accessible Canada Act compliance
  • Data Protection: UPS battery backup for workstations

Safety Protocols

Compliant

Mandatory 30-minute safety orientation for all participants. Hazard classes defined per discipline with dual-supervisor coverage for high-risk equipment.

Hazard Classes
  • Robotics/Electronics: Soldering ventilation, ESD-safe stations, battery fire-safe storage, eye protection
  • Digital Fabrication: CNC machine guarding, dust collection, laser safety enclosure, face shields
  • Software Dev: Standard ergonomics; no high-risk equipment; UPS backup
  • Creative/Applied Design: Adhesive/spray ventilation, blade safety, proper lighting

Technical feasibility is confirmed.

6.2 Updated Cost Analysis - 2026 Adjustment

An independent analysis of Langford, British Columbia’s 2026 economic conditions necessitates an adjustment of the original March 2026 startup estimates. Inflationary pressures in the West Shore commercial real estate market and provincial wage adjustments require a recalibration of initial assumptions.

Cost Adjustments and Identifications

Underestimated Costs: Initial personnel costs ($110,000 for 2 FTEs + Admin) were sub-market for 2026. Retaining qualified, safety-certified technical instructors in BC requires competitive compensation (estimated $65,000/year per Lead Instructor) plus a 15-20% load for mandatory benefits (WorkSafeBC premiums, CPP, EI). Furthermore, commercial lease estimates must be adjusted to $28-$30/sqft gross to accurately reflect recent municipal property tax impacts on Triple Net (NNN) leases.

Overestimated Costs: The initial $10,000-$20,000 marketing budget can be reduced. Organic community demand and strategic partnerships with local school districts will yield high initial conversion rates with a leaner digital marketing spend.

Missing Cost Categories: The original budget omitted an equipment depreciation/sinking fund and explicit accounting for commercial general liability (CGL) insurance riders specific to public-access high-risk machinery.

6.3.1 Break-Even Discussion & Revenue Assumptions

Under the adjusted Lean Model, monthly operating expenses average approximately $25,833. To achieve a sustainable surplus for the contingency reserve, monthly revenue must target $29,000.

To meet this, the original membership pricing assumption must shift. The target becomes 200 active members at $60/month ($12,000), supported by $17,000 monthly distributed across workshop fees, corporate training contracts, institutional partnerships, and sustained grant funding. The commitment to capping any single funding source at 30% of total revenue remains intact, ensuring the center is shielded from abrupt grant cancellations.

Note: The original project proposal (March 11, 2026) projected $34,000 monthly revenue at $50/month with 160 members. The figures above reflect the March 26 updated cost analysis, which adjusted membership pricing to $60/month and the member target to 200 to account for 2026 market conditions.

6.4.2 Price Sensitivity

The community survey found that 61% of respondents preferred a monthly membership fee in the $25-$50 range. The updated $60/month base fee exceeds this preference band, making the sliding-scale structure essential for maintaining equitable access. The $15 drop-in option provides a lower-commitment entry point for price-sensitive users.

6.4.3 Community Ecosystem

The volunteer mentorship cohort, projected at 15 to 25 active members contributing approximately 200 hours monthly, fosters a peer-support ecosystem. This model anchors the facility as a vital “third place” for community development, blending structured learning with social connectivity.

Social feasibility remains highly robust.

6.5 Environmental and Cultural Feasibility

6.5.1 Environmental Considerations

Light fabrication waste is managed via standard commercial protocols. Discipline-specific waste management includes:

  • Digital Fabrication: Dust collection on CNC router; 3D printer waste (PLA, PETG) recyclable through commercial programs; laser cutter enclosed with fume extraction.
  • Robotics/Electronics: Soldering fume extraction; battery fire-safe storage; e-waste managed through BC-certified recycling.
  • Creative/Applied Design: Ventilation for adhesives and sprays; physical modelling scrap via standard commercial waste.

Iterative prototyping inherently minimizes raw material waste, as designs are refined digitally before physical fabrication.

6.5.2 Cultural Alignment

The British Columbia Ministry of Education’s ADST curriculum emphasizes experiential learning and iterative design across K-12 education. Crossroads directly extends the ADST model into the community setting. The facility addresses the infrastructure deficit caused by rapid municipal expansion in the West Shore, where population growth (3.95% year-over-year) has outpaced community learning infrastructure development.

Environmental and cultural feasibility is confirmed.

6.6 Political and Legal Feasibility

6.6.1 Regulatory Roadmap

  1. BC Societies Act: Incorporation as a nonprofit society to maximize public trust and grant eligibility. Bylaws are a deliverable of the Detailed Design phase.
  2. WorkSafeBC Compliance: Safety orientation protocols, dual-supervisor coverage, premium payments for workshop environments.
  3. CGL Insurance: Specialized riders for open-access facility with CO2 lasers, CNC mills, and soldering stations. Lean budget: $28,000 (up from $20,000 in original proposal).
  4. Accessible Canada Act: Full facility compliance for accessibility.

8. Conclusion and Recommendation

This feasibility study confirms that the Crossroads Community STEM Centre remains viable in the 2026 economic landscape. While regional market pressures in Langford - specifically commercial real estate rates ($28-$30/sqft gross) and professional wage benchmarks ($65,000/year per Lead Instructor) - have increased the Year 1 Lean Model startup requirement to $476,100 (up 32.2% from the original $359,950 estimate), the underlying demand for accessible technical education is thoroughly validated.
